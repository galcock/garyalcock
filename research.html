Research

Density Field Dynamics & precision tests of gravity.

My physics work centers on Density Field Dynamics (DFD), a scalar-refractive framework where a single field ψ(x) sets both the optical index and test-mass accelerations, with explicit predictions for optical clocks, gravitational waves, and galactic dynamics.

Primary archive: densityfielddynamics.com

Working summary

DFD replaces curved spacetime with a scalar field ψ on flat ℝ³. Photons see an optical metric via n = eψ (so c₁ = c e−ψ), and matter accelerates as a = (c²/2)∇ψ. The framework is tuned to match GR’s classic tests while making falsifiable predictions in cross-sector experiments.

The near-term goal is to either confirm or decisively falsify the ψ-based description using optical clocks, cavities, atom interferometers, and astrophysical data.

Current lines of work

Optical clocks & LPI

Re-analysis of long-baseline optical clock comparisons (including ROCIT 2022) to test whether photons and atoms respond identically to the same gravitational / energy-density environment, and how any deviation would manifest.

Work includes fits to heliocentric templates, block-permutation significance testing, and proposals for height-separated cavity–atom experiments that isolate geometry-locked effects.

Cavity–atom LPI protocols

A sector-resolved Local Position Invariance test where evacuated optical cavities and co-located atomic clocks are compared at two different geopotentials. GR predicts a null result; DFD predicts a geometry-locked slope.

Emphasis on materials and species combinations (e.g., ULE vs Si, Sr vs Yb) and careful control of dispersion, thermal, and mechanical systematics.

PPN & strong-field sector

Mapping DFD into the parametrized post-Newtonian (PPN) framework and extending to strong-field and gravitational-wave regimes, to clarify exactly where the ψ-theory tracks GR and where it diverges.

Goal: make it straightforward for gravitational wave and strong-field communities to test DFD using existing data and ppE-style parametrizations.

Galactic dynamics & cosmology

Using the ψ-field equation with a MOND-like crossover function μ(x) to account for flat rotation curves and the radial acceleration relation without invoking particle dark matter, and studying how line-of-sight refractive effects bias local H₀ measurements.

Particular interest in whether local H₀ anisotropies correlate with density gradients along the line of sight.
